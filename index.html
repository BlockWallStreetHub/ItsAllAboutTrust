Section 01

Foundations of Trust

Before you can judge any monetary system — gold vault or blockchain — you have to answer one question: when you "own" something inside it, what do you actually hold?

Custody vs. Credit: The Only Distinction That Matters

Every financial arrangement in history sorts into two buckets. In a custody relationship, an asset is held for you — segregated, titled to you, returnable on demand. If the custodian fails, the asset is still yours; creditors can't touch it. In a credit relationship, you've handed the asset over and received a promise in return. You are now a creditor. If the counterparty fails, you stand in line.

The danger is that both feel identical on a screen. A number in an app looks the same whether it represents allocated metal in a vault or an IOU from a company that lent your metal out. Celsius depositors in 2022 learned this in bankruptcy court: "my coins earning yield" turned out to mean "unsecured loans to a failed lender." The same distinction has destroyed gold depositors for centuries — it's why goldsmith receipts became fractional reserve banking became bank runs.

The Legal Machinery: Trusts, Trustees, and Escrow

A trust is the oldest custody technology in Anglo-American law. One party (the grantor or settlor) transfers assets to a trustee, who holds legal title but must manage the assets for the beneficiary's benefit under a fiduciary duty — the highest duty the law imposes. A revocable grantor trust can be amended or dissolved by the grantor at any time; for tax purposes the grantor is still treated as the owner. An irrevocable trust is a one-way door: assets leave the grantor's estate and control.

An escrow agent is a neutral third party who holds assets mid-transaction and releases them only when conditions are met. Escrow is transactional custody; a trust is standing custody. Sophisticated systems chain them: money enters through escrow, converts, then rests in trust.

The person who creates and funds a trust. In a revocable trust, retains full control and tax ownership.

Why Concentration of Roles Matters

Trust law works because roles check each other: the trustee answers to beneficiaries, auditors verify the trustee, courts enforce the duty. When one party plays multiple roles — drafter of the documents, trustee of the assets, counsel to the organization, owner of the service providers — the checks collapse into disclosures and waivers. That isn't automatically fraud; disclosed concentration is legal and common. But it converts structural protection into reputational protection, and the reader of the documents needs to know which one they're relying on.

The BWS Rule

In crypto we say "don't trust, verify" and "not your keys, not your coins." Traditional finance has the same rules in older language: read the trust instrument, identify the trustee, and ask who holds your asset when things go wrong. Custody vs. credit is not an asset-class question. It's the question, for every asset class.

1.1You deposit gold with a firm that promises "your gold, plus 3% yield." The firm lends your gold to fund its operations. What do you now hold?
Yield for possession = custody converted to credit. If the firm fails, you stand in the creditor line. The 2022 crypto lenders and centuries of goldsmith banking both prove the pattern.
1.2What is the defining feature of a revocable grantor trust?
Revocable = grantor keeps control and tax ownership. It organizes and titles assets; it does not shield them from the grantor's taxes or creditors the way an irrevocable trust can.
1.3Which situation preserves the strongest structural protection for the asset owner?
Separated roles check each other. Every other option replaces structural checks with disclosure, waivers, or reputation — which can be acceptable, but is weaker, and you should know you've made that trade.
1.4Why is "unallocated" a loaded word in precious metals?
Allocated vs. unallocated is the metals version of self-custody vs. exchange balance. A fully-reserved fungible pool is different from a true unallocated claim — but the burden is on the documents to prove which one you have.
1.5The crypto principle "not your keys, not your coins" maps to which trust-law question?
Both traditions ask the identical question: in failure, is the asset yours or are you in line? Custody vs. credit — the only distinction that matters.
Section 02

The Constitutional Case for Specie

UPMA's entire architecture rests on a 235-year-old carve-out: states can't coin money — but the Constitution explicitly names the one thing they CAN make legal tender.

Article I: A Prohibition With a Door In It

Article I, Section 10 says no state shall "coin Money" nor "make any Thing but gold and silver Coin a Tender in Payment of Debts." Read one way, it's pure prohibition. Read carefully, it's an affirmative grant: gold and silver coin are the single exception — the one form of money states retain constitutional authority over. Section 8 gives Congress the coining power; Section 10 leaves states the specie-tender lane. Vertical checks and balances, applied to money itself.

Lane County v. Oregon (1868): The Load-Bearing Precedent

During the greenback era, Oregon required taxes paid in gold and silver coin. The Supreme Court upheld it: a state's power to specify what it accepts for taxes is an original power, never surrendered to the federal government. Every modern specie legal tender statute stands on this case — and every specie tax payment to a state is a live exercise of it.

The Modern Wave

Utah's Specie Legal Tender Act (2011) recognized U.S.-minted gold and silver coin as legal tender in Utah and, in 2012, exempted specie transactions from state sales tax — treating a coin-for-goods exchange as currency use, not a property sale. Arizona, Colorado, Missouri, Oklahoma, and Wyoming followed with their own recognition statutes, and the movement has kept expanding: dozens of states have since removed sales taxes on bullion, several have exempted specie gains from state income tax, and Texas built a state bullion depository. UPMA was founded in direct response to the Utah act — the association is the operating layer built on the statute.

Digital Asset Parallel

This is the same playbook as state-level Bitcoin reserve bills and crypto tax carve-outs: when the federal monetary layer is closed to change, innovation routes through federalism. Lane County — a state choosing what it accepts for taxes — is the same legal principle behind Colorado accepting crypto for tax payments today.

The Hard Boundary

State recognition changes state treatment only. Federally, the IRS classifies gold coins as property (collectibles) — spending appreciated specie is a taxable disposition at fair market value no matter what any state statute calls it. Section 09 covers exactly where this line sits and what happens to people who cross it.

2.1What does Article I, Section 10 actually permit states to do?
Coining stays with Congress (Sec. 8). But the tender clause's own text carves out gold and silver coin as the one thing states may make legal tender.
2.2Lane County v. Oregon (1868) established that:
Oregon could demand gold and silver for taxes even in the greenback era. This is the precedent every modern specie legal tender law is built on.
2.3What practical friction did Utah's 2012 sales-tax provision remove for specie users?
At the state level, spending specie stopped being a taxed property disposition. Critically, this changed nothing at the federal level.
2.4Why was UPMA founded when it was?
Statute first, infrastructure second. The 2011 Utah act created the legal category; UPMA was organized to make it usable in daily life.
2.5The closest digital-asset parallel to the state specie movement is:
Both are monetary innovation routing through federalism when the federal layer won't move. Same strategy, different asset.
Section 03

The Intent Doctrine & Gold Clauses

The same coin can be money or property — and which one it is depends on what you're doing with it. That functional test is the intellectual heart of the entire system.

Thorne: Use Determines Character

In Thorne & Wilson v. Utah State Tax Commission (1984), following Michigan National Bank v. Dept. of Treasury, the Utah Supreme Court drew a line that still governs: coins transferred as a medium of exchange remain money (intangible — no sales tax; you can't be taxed for buying money with money). Coins transferred as an investment commodity become tangible personal property, and the sale is taxable. Remarkably, Thorne applied this even to Krugerrands — foreign coins with no U.S. legal tender status — during an era when the U.S. wasn't minting specie tender at all. Monetary character came from use and intent, not from a statute.

The doctrine cuts both ways. In U.S. ex rel. Holbrook v. Brink's (2015), coins that nominally were legal tender lost monetary status because they were traded on their metal value rather than used as tender. Face value matters less than transactional behavior.

Why this shapes UPMA's design: every feature that makes specie circulate — peer-to-peer transfers, merchant payments, tax payments at 0% fee, dues denominated in gold cents — also manufactures an evidentiary record of monetary use. The network isn't just a product; it's a standing argument that its members' specie is money-in-use under the Thorne test.

Gold Clauses: Contracting Out of "A Dollar Is a Dollar"

Federal law is nominalist: for paying ordinary debts, "a dollar is a dollar regardless of the physical embodiment" (Crummey v. Klein, 5th Cir. 2008, following Thompson v. Butler, 1878). Tender a $50 Gold Eagle against a debt and it discharges exactly $50 — the courts have never let anyone monetize the face-value gap, in either direction.

But parties can contract out of nominalism. The post-Civil War Supreme Court (Bronson v. Rodes, Butler v. Horwitz, 1869) enforced contracts requiring payment in gold coin. Congress voided such "gold clauses" in 1933 mid-devaluation — then quietly re-legalized them for obligations entered after October 27, 1977 (31 U.S.C. § 5118). Modern courts enforce them with teeth: the Sixth Circuit's 216 Jamaica Avenue case (2008) revived a 1912 lease's gold clause and multiplied the rent obligation.

UPMA embeds a gold clause into every member-to-member transaction on its exchange: settlement in Gold Dollars first, then Silver Dollars. Every internal sale is a statutorily protected specie obligation — and one more entry in the monetary-use record.

The Reframe That Matters

The gap between a Gold Eagle's $50 face and its ~$4,000+ market value is not a loophole. It is the measurement of the argument — a running, quantified record of the paper dollar's lost purchasing power. The gap is the thesis, not the trade. Anyone who tells you it's a tax trade is walking you into Section 09.

3.1Under Thorne, what determines whether a coin transaction is taxed as a property sale?
A functional test: use as money = intangible, untaxed exchange. Use as commodity = tangible property, taxable sale. Same coin, different character.
3.2What happened to gold clauses in 1933 and 1977?
The 1933 Joint Resolution killed them during devaluation; a quiet 1977 amendment revived them. For nearly fifty years it has been legal to contract for payment in gold — a right almost nobody uses.
3.3In Crummey v. Klein, the sound-money litigant argued his gold coins should be credited at market value. The court:
Nominalism consistently defeats attempts to exploit the face-value gap — in both directions. Tender at face; taxed at market. The asymmetry is the trap.
3.4Why does UPMA design features like 0%-fee tax payments and gold-denominated dues?
The architecture is designed to win a functional legal test. Circulation isn't just the mission — it's the evidence.
3.5The honest way to describe the gap between a Gold Eagle's face value and market value:
The gap measures the argument. Treating it as an exploitable trade is the frivolous position covered in Section 09 — and it has a 100% loss rate in court.

The Private Membership Wrapper

Before the entities, understand the perimeter they all live inside. UPMA operates as a private membership association in function: services are offered only to enrolled members who have contractually agreed to terms, dues, and dispute-resolution provisions — never to the general public. The theory behind PMA structures draws on the First Amendment freedom of association (NAACP v. Alabama and its line): private members conducting private affairs among themselves occupy a different legal posture than a business soliciting the public. That's why everything here is member-to-member — the exchange, the transfers, the merchant network, even the 0% spread is framed as members exchanging with the membership, not a dealer quoting the street.

What the private wrapper realistically does: it keeps transactions off public retail rails, supports the no-routine-KYC privacy posture, narrows who can even bring a dispute (members bound by venue, mediation, and confidentiality clauses), and reinforces the "not a financial institution, not a dealer" characterization — you can't be a dealer to the public if the public can't buy from you.

What it does not do — and this is where PMA folklore gets people hurt: private membership is not an immunity spell. Courts have consistently held that PMA status does not exempt an organization from generally applicable law — tax obligations, securities law, health and safety codes, criminal statutes. People have gone to prison operating "private member" medical practices, food operations, and investment clubs on the theory that the wrapper placed them outside jurisdiction. It doesn't. Notice that UPMA's own lawyers clearly know this: rather than resting on bare PMA theory, they built a registered Utah Limited Cooperative Association with 501(c)(6) federal recognition, OFAC compliance representations, anti-tax-shelter clauses, and escrow for all fiat touchpoints. The private membership design is one disciplined layer of a lawful structure — not a claim to be beyond the law. When you hear anyone, in any system, say "we're a PMA so [regulation/tax/reporting] doesn't apply," you are listening to the folklore register, not the paper.

Fees, Premiums & the Liquidity Gate Stack

"0% buy/sell spread" is true — inside a window, at a marked-up rate, subject to suspension. The honest picture is a stack of gates, each reasonable alone, that together define what your money can actually do.

The 0% Spread, Precisely Stated

Buy $2,000 of vaulted specie today, redeem $2,000 tomorrow (rate unchanged) — genuinely better than any coin dealer's two-sided spread. But three qualifiers live in the fine print: it applies up to roughly $10,000 per rolling month (exchanges and redemptions combined against the same threshold); it can be suspended in extreme market conditions — precisely when you'd want it most; and the daily rate you transact at is not spot. Historical premiums: gold ~5.8% over spot, silver ~20%, Goldbacks spot + $1.00 production + 10%. You buy and sell at the same marked-up rate — the entry premium is real and recoverable only by exiting through the same window. Above the monthly threshold: ~1% spread on excess, Goldbacks haircut 10% from $10K–$100K, and above $100K it's "call for a quote depending on the liquidity condition of the network" — the honest admission that Goldbacks have no deep external market.

Privacy by Structure

Because the association is "not a financial institution," it collects no SSN for ordinary membership — identity verification appears only at pressure points: physical withdrawal (photo ID), address changes (proof of residency), name changes, death transfers, and the debit card (where the issuing bank's KYC, including SSN, applies). What privacy is not: a tax feature. "They don't have the number to report" describes an information-reporting gap. Your self-reporting obligations exist regardless of whether anyone files a form about you — Section 09 is mandatory reading before drawing any comfort from this design.

The Succession Machinery

Membership itself is non-transferable and dies with the member — nothing succeeds to it by operation of law. What transfers is the account value, through three designable roles:

JOINT TENANT
Rights equal to the primary holder and superior to everyone else, during life and after. Added online; removable only by phone with documentation. Functionally a near-irrevocable gift of full control — spouses and true co-owners only.
BENEFICIARY
Inherits a designated percentage on death (absent a Joint Tenant), upon death certificate. Add and remove freely online.
AGENT
Acts on the account under a power of attorney or trust document; holds no beneficial interest. The clean role for trust-based structures.

All three roles must be existing members (funded or not) — estate planning here means onboarding your heirs first.

Section 09

The Tax Boundary

Everything in this system survives contact with federal law except one idea — and that idea carries a $5,000 automatic penalty, felony convictions, and a 100% loss rate in court.

The Face-Value Position, Named and Condemned

IRS Internal Revenue Bulletin 2010-17, frivolous position #13: claiming that in a gold or silver coin transaction, the amount realized is the coins' face value rather than fair market value. Under 26 U.S.C. § 6702, filing on that basis triggers an automatic $5,000 penalty per submission unless withdrawn within 30 days of notice. And the enforcement record is worse than the penalty: Robert Kahre paid workers in Eagles at face value for years, reported accordingly, and was convicted on dozens of federal counts — 15 years. Courts have held, uniformly and without exception, that income received in gold is measured at fair market value on receipt.

The seductive version of the argument goes: nominalism says a dollar is a dollar (Thompson, Crummey) — so if the IRS must credit a Silver Eagle at $1 when tendered, symmetry demands they can only tax it at $1 when received. But nominalism governs tender and discharge of debts; it has never governed income measurement, which runs on fair market value, full stop. Notice the asymmetry cuts against the holder both ways: tender an Eagle, credited $50; receive an Eagle, taxed at $4,000+. Everyone who has litigated the gap — in either direction — has lost.

And the "nobody's tracking it, report it if you want" version is worse than wrong: structuring around non-reporting is precisely what converts a civil position into criminal willfulness. Kahre's sentence wasn't for a legal misunderstanding — it was for a system designed around the gap.

What Actually Survives

Legitimate — use freely

  • Gresham's strategy: spend paper, save specie. A savings behavior, not a tax position.
  • Gold clauses: contracting to be paid in specie (post-1977) — enforceable, but income is still FMV.
  • State-layer treatment: sales-tax exemptions, state income-tax carve-outs, the Thorne intent doctrine.
  • Collateralize, don't sell: loans are not dispositions. The GoldELOC and BTC-backed borrowing are the compliant answer to "I need liquidity but won't sell."

Radioactive — never

  • Reporting specie income at face value (frivolous position #13; $5,000 per filing)
  • Not reporting because "there's no SSN / no form / no tracking" (the willfulness trap)
  • Treating card loads of appreciated metal as non-events (each load is a disposition at FMV)
  • Taking tax theory from evangelists whose own contracts contain "this is not an abusive tax shelter" clauses disavowing it
